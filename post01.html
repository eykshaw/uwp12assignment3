1. Introduction

December 1, 2018

Podcasting is an ever growing entertainment medium, unique to the rise of the internet. Rebecca Mead of The New Yorker (2018) defines a podcast as "on-demand audio that a listener can download and play while commuting or exercising or, given the right equipment, showering." This medium contains many genres, but in this series of blog posts, we will look at the architecture history podcast genre.

Rhetorical Situation

The rhetorical situation, or set of circumstances in which the piece exists, comprises the audience, purpose, context, and genre (Arola et al, 2014). The intended audience contains a range of people; it is composed of professional architects, architecture students, and people who simply have an interest in architectural design. This type of podcast's primary purpose is to provide entertainment to the listener. Its secondary purpose is to educate the audience on architectural history and concepts. The context in which the podcast will be interacted with is usually in a casual, individual setting. As mentioned previously, the genre is that of the architecture history podcast.

In this analysis, we must acknowledge that this podcast genre contains a great range in the production of the podcast itself. Some podcasts are part of a larger network, which allows access to more editing and producing assistance, while some are independent projects, edited and produced by a single person or just a few people. Our analysis will discuss the various qualities of each podcast without accounting for this variable.

Examples

How well a podcast fulfills the expectations of its genre is essential in maintaining an intimate and lasting relationship with the listener. The next three posts will explore how this is achieved through the use of the visual (cover art), aural (sound and music), and linguistic (language and delivery) modes of communication in relation to appeals to ethos, pathos, and logos.
